# Abstract Geometries

**Curated by**: Digital Aesthetics Manifesto Community  
**Opening**: November 19, 2025  
**Theme**: Non-representational pattern and form in ASCII

## Curatorial Statement

Not all ASCII art attempts to represent something. Some works embrace abstraction—pure pattern, pure form, pure rhythm. These pieces don't depict mountains or faces or machines. Instead, they explore what happens when characters themselves become the subject.

Abstract ASCII art has its own logic. Repetition creates rhythm. Symmetry creates stability. Variation creates tension. The grid structure of monospaced text naturally suggests geometric patterns, making ASCII particularly suited to abstract work.

This exhibition celebrates ASCII art that has no "subject" except its own formal properties—works that ask you to see characters not as symbols with meaning, but as shapes, densities, textures. In these pieces, the medium isn't trying to be something else. The medium is the message.

## Understanding Abstraction

### Why Abstract ASCII?

**Grid Logic**: ASCII's monospaced grid naturally suggests geometric patterns. Working with the grid rather than against it creates harmony.

**Pure Form**: Without representational goals, artists can focus entirely on shape, rhythm, density, repetition.

**Algorithmic**: Many abstract pieces are algorithmically generated or follow strict rules, blurring the line between art and code.

**Meditative**: Creating or viewing abstract patterns can be meditative—the mind focuses on pure visual input without narrative.

**Accessible**: Abstract work doesn't require technical skill to render recognizable subjects. Anyone can make patterns.

### Common Structures

**Symmetry**: Mirror, radial, or rotational symmetry creates stability and balance.

**Gradients**: Progressive change in density, spacing, or character choice.

**Repetition**: Same element repeated creates rhythm; variations create interest.

**Interference**: Overlapping patterns create moiré and other optical effects.

**Fractals**: Self-similar patterns at different scales.

**Random**: Pseudo-random arrangements that appear chaotic but follow rules.

### Techniques

**Single Character**: Using one character only—pattern comes from spacing and arrangement.

**Two Characters**: High contrast—black/white, solid/void, mark/space.

**Density Gradients**: Multiple characters ordered by visual weight (. : ; + * # @ █).

**Unicode Shading**: Using ░▒▓█ blocks for four gray levels.

**Mathematical**: Following geometric or algebraic rules to generate pattern.

## Historical Context

Abstract ASCII art has different origins than representational ASCII art:

**1960s-70s**: Computer-generated abstract art using line printers. Artists like Frieder Nake and Georg Nees created algorithmic works printed as ASCII.

**1980s**: Abstract patterns in demo scene—scrolling text, pattern fields, transitions.

**1990s**: Abstract signatures and decorative elements in BBS art.

**2000s**: Code poetry and text art exploring ASCII as medium rather than tool.

**2010s+**: Return to abstraction as reaction against photorealistic ASCII converters—hand-crafted pattern work reasserts human creativity.

Unlike representational ASCII, abstract ASCII is often created by programmers and mathematicians rather than visual artists. The work sits at the intersection of art, code, and mathematics.
