# The Golden Age: 1990s ASCII Art

**Curated by**: Digital Aesthetics Manifesto Community  
**Opening**: November 19, 2025  
**Theme**: Historical survey of BBS and early internet ASCII art

## Curatorial Statement

The 1990s were the golden age of ASCII art. Bulletin Board Systems (BBS) connected people through dial-up modems, and text-based communication was the norm. Email signatures, BBS welcome screens, and newsgroup posts provided canvases for ASCII artists.

This was before broadband, before images loaded instantly. ASCII art wasn't just decorationâ€”it was the only way to add visual interest to text-only environments. Artists like Joan G. Stark (jgs), Normand Veilleux (nv), and countless anonymous contributors developed sophisticated techniques that are still used today.

This exhibition surveys the 1990s ASCII art scene, showcasing representative works that defined the era. These pieces demonstrate the technical mastery, creativity, and community spirit that made this decade special.

## The 1990s Scene

### Key Artists

**Joan G. Stark (jgs)**: Most prolific and influential. Created thousands of pieces from 1995-2003. Known for clean lines, perfect symmetry, and remarkable rangeâ€”from cute animals to holiday themes to complex landscapes.

**Normand Veilleux (nv)**: Canadian artist who created elaborate fantasy scenes and technical diagrams.

**Hayley Jane Wakenshaw (hjw)**: British artist known for detailed animals and flowers.

**Countless anonymous contributors**: Most 1990s ASCII art was created by anonymous BBS users and email enthusiasts.

### Distribution

**Email signatures**: The primary distribution method. Artists would sign emails with ASCII art, which would be seen by recipients and often copied.

**Usenet newsgroups**: `alt.ascii-art` and related groups were hubs for sharing and discussion.

**BBS file areas**: ASCII art collections could be downloaded as text files.

**Web archives**: By late 1990s, websites like Chris.com collected and categorized ASCII art.

**Printed collections**: Some ASCII art was compiled into books and zines.

### Tools

**Text editors**: Most artists used simple text editorsâ€”Notepad, vi, pico.

**Banner programs**: Tools like `figlet` created large block letters.

**Character maps**: Artists would reference ASCII or extended ASCII charts to find useful characters.

**Trial and error**: No WYSIWYG previewâ€”artists typed and checked spacing manually.

### Cultural Context

**Modem culture**: 2400-56k baud connections made images impractical. ASCII art was functional necessity.

**Hacker aesthetic**: Text-based interfaces were seen as more "authentic" than GUIsâ€”ASCII art fit the ethos.

**Community**: BBS and newsgroup communities were tight-knit. ASCII art signatures were identity markers.

**Pre-commercial web**: Before banner ads and corporate websites, the internet was largely text-based and creative.

## Why the Golden Age Ended

By the early 2000s, several factors diminished ASCII art's prominence:

**Broadband**: Fast connections made images practicalâ€”photos replaced ASCII art.

**HTML email**: Rich formatting and embedded images made plain text signatures obsolete.

**Social media**: Platforms like Facebook used profile photos, not text signatures.

**Character encoding**: UTF-8 replaced ASCIIâ€”older art using extended ASCII broke.

**Cultural shift**: The hacker/BBS culture gave way to mainstream internet use.

However, ASCII art never truly died. It lives on in:
- Terminal UIs and command-line tools
- Retro gaming and demo scenes
- Programming tutorials and documentation
- Art projects exploring constraint and nostalgia
- This manifesto and exhibitions like this one

## Legacy

The 1990s established techniques still used today:

**Character selection**: Using visual weight (. : + * # @) for shading
**Line construction**: Building curves from straight-edge characters
**Negative space**: Using absence as important as presence
**Signature style**: Small, personal ASCII art as identity marker
**Community archives**: Collaborative collections and attribution

Artists like Joan Stark influenced every ASCII artist who came after. Her work is still discovered by new generations, and her techniques are still taught.
