# Portraits in Monochrome

**Curated by**: Digital Aesthetics Manifesto Community  
**Opening**: November 19, 2025  
**Theme**: Human faces and figures rendered in ASCII

## Curatorial Statement

The human face is one of the most challenging subjects for any artist—and doubly so for ASCII artists working with a grid of monospaced characters. How do you capture the curve of a cheek, the glint in an eye, the expression of a smile, using only straight-edged characters locked to a fixed grid?

This exhibition showcases ASCII portraits that tackle this challenge head-on. Some artists embrace caricature, exaggerating features for comic or expressive effect. Others pursue photorealism, using density gradients to approximate photographic values. Still others find a middle path—stylized but recognizable, schematic but emotional.

What unites these works is the artists' refusal to accept that text characters are "just" for writing. In these portraits, characters become pixels, letters become light and shadow, and the limitations of the medium become opportunities for invention.

## Techniques Analysis

### Character Selection for Faces

**Skin tones**:
- Light: @ ░ (spaces between characters)
- Medium: ▒ ≈ ~
- Dark: ▓ # █

**Hair**:
- Straight: | I ∥
- Curly: @ % ∼
- Wild: * ∗ ✱

**Eyes**:
- Detailed: oO ⊙ ◉
- Simple: o O 0
- Closed: - ~ =

**Mouth**:
- Smile: ) ⌣ ∪
- Sad: ( ⌢ ∩
- Open: ○ ◯
- Laughing: w W ω

### Common Approaches

**Photorealistic**: Attempts to render faces with photographic fidelity using density gradients. Requires large canvas (40×50+ characters) and careful value control.

**Caricature**: Exaggerates distinctive features—large eyes, prominent noses, expressive mouths. Often more recognizable than realistic attempts.

**Silhouette**: Profile or frontal silhouettes with no interior detail. Relies entirely on outline and negative space.

**Schematic**: Simplified to essential features. Eyes, nose, mouth clearly indicated but not detailed. Most common approach.

**Abstract**: Face suggested through pattern and composition rather than rendering. Least literal, most interpretive.

## The Challenge of Portraits

Why are faces so difficult in ASCII?

1. **Grid vs. Curve**: Faces are all curves—the ASCII grid is all straight edges. Artists must fake curves through careful stepping and anti-aliasing effects.

2. **Expression**: Tiny changes in eye or mouth position create different emotions. With grid-locked characters, subtle expression is nearly impossible—artists must exaggerate.

3. **Recognition**: Humans are excellent at face recognition and terrible at ignoring slight errors. An ASCII face that's 95% correct looks completely wrong.

4. **Individuality**: Generic faces are easy—specific people are hard. Capturing what makes Einstein Einstein or your grandmother your grandmother is the real challenge.

5. **Scale**: Small faces lack detail for features. Large faces require extensive canvases and can appear empty or overly pixelated.

Despite these challenges, ASCII artists persist. Why? Perhaps because the difficulty makes success more rewarding. Or perhaps because the medium's limitations force artists to find creative solutions impossible in unrestricted media.

## Historical Note

ASCII portraits have appeared since the earliest days of computer art:
- 1960s: Computer-generated ASCII portraits from image data
- 1970s-80s: Hand-crafted portraits in print art
- 1990s: BBS signature art and email portraits
- 2000s: Automated ASCII converters (PhotoASCII, ASCII Art Studio)
- 2010s+: Return to hand-crafted portraits as art form distinct from automated conversion

The tension between hand-crafted and computer-generated ASCII portraits remains. Most artists use hybrid approaches—automated conversion to establish values, then hand-refinement for expression and clarity.
