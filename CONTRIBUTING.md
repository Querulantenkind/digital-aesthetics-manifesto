# Contributing to Digital Aesthetics Manifesto

Thank you for your interest in contributing to this project. This document explains how to participate, what kinds of contributions we welcome, and the process for submitting work.

## Philosophy of Contribution

This project operates on principles of:
- **Openness**: All contributions are welcome, regardless of technical expertise
- **Diversity**: We seek voices from different backgrounds, regions, and perspectives
- **Quality over quantity**: Thoughtful work matters more than volume
- **Collaboration**: Building together strengthens the work
- **Attribution**: Credit is given where due, anonymity respected where desired

---

## What We Welcome

### Essays and Theoretical Work

**Philosophical essays** exploring:
- Anonymity, identity, and digital selfhood
- Aesthetics of text-based media
- Terminal culture and computing practices
- Resistance through technology
- Alternative digital futures

**Critical analysis** of:
- Digital platforms and their politics
- Design trends and assumptions
- Historical computing practices
- Contemporary digital culture

**Length**: 2,000-8,000 words typically, but flexible based on content  
**Style**: Accessible academic writing—rigorous but readable  
**Format**: Markdown with proper citations

---

### Case Studies

**Historical investigations** into:
- BBS culture, zines, underground networks
- Early internet aesthetics
- Hacker and phreaker movements
- Regional computing cultures

**Contemporary analysis** of:
- Current digital practices
- Emerging aesthetic movements
- Community-driven projects
- Alternative platforms and tools

**Structure**: Context, analysis, significance, sources  
**Length**: 3,000-6,000 words  
**Requirements**: Primary sources, interviews, or direct experience preferred

---

### Art and Creative Work

**ASCII art**: Original works or historical pieces (with attribution)

**Unicode art**: Experimental symbol-based artwork

**Terminal designs**: Interface mockups, color schemes, layouts

**Documentation as art**: Creative approaches to technical writing

**Code poetry**: Programs that are simultaneously functional and poetic

**Requirements**:
- Original work or properly attributed
- Include technical details (character set, dimensions, tools used)
- Artist statement explaining concept and approach
- High-resolution or plain text format

---

### Manifestos

**Personal manifestos** declaring your:
- Relationship to technology
- Aesthetic principles
- Political commitments
- Creative practices

**Collective manifestos** from:
- Communities or organizations
- Regional groups
- Disciplinary fields
- Collaborative projects

**Guidelines**:
- Clear, declarative statements ("We believe...", "We reject...", "We demand...")
- Specific and actionable when possible
- Length: 500-2,000 words
- Bold, opinionated, sincere

---

### Interviews

**Subjects we seek**:
- Digital artists and net artists
- Hackers and security researchers
- Zine makers and publishers
- Activists and organizers
- Designers and typographers
- Writers and poets
- Anyone with unique digital practice

**Format**:
- Q&A or conversational essay
- 3,000-6,000 words
- Include brief bio of interviewee
- Obtain written consent for publication

---

### Toolkits and Guides

**Practical how-tos** for:
- Creating specific types of art
- Using particular tools or technologies
- Building communities or projects
- Organizing events or exhibitions

**Requirements**:
- Clear, step-by-step instructions
- Assume minimal prior knowledge
- Include examples and screenshots/diagrams where helpful
- Recommend free/open source tools when possible
- Length: 2,000-5,000 words

---

### Creative Exercises

**Prompts and projects** for:
- Writers exploring digital themes
- Designers working with constraints
- Programmers creating expressively
- Activists organizing and resisting
- Collaborative group work

**Structure**:
- Concept and goals
- Materials needed
- Step-by-step instructions
- Variations and extensions
- Reflection prompts

### Translations

We enthusiastically welcome translations into other languages. 

**Process**:
1. Open an issue announcing which document you'll translate
2. Create folder: `/translations/[language-code]/`
3. Translate maintaining markdown formatting
4. Submit pull request
5. Include your name in translator credits

**Languages especially sought**: Spanish, French, German, Mandarin, Arabic, Portuguese, Japanese, Russian, Hindi

### Resources and Links

**Additions to resource lists**:
- Books and articles
- Tools and software
- Communities and organizations
- Documentaries and media
- Conferences and events

**Requirements**:
- Brief annotation explaining relevance
- Working link (verify before submitting)
- Appropriate categorization

## Technical Guidelines

### File Formats

- **Text**: Markdown (.md) with standard formatting
- **Images**: PNG or JPG, reasonable file size (<5MB typically)
- **ASCII art**: Plain text (.txt) or in markdown code blocks
- **Code**: Include language identifier in code blocks

### Markdown Style

```markdown
# Top-level heading (one per document)

## Section headings

### Subsection headings

**Bold** for emphasis, *italic* for terms

- Bullet lists for items
- Consistent formatting

> Block quotes for citations

Code blocks with language:
```python
print("Hello, world!")
```

Links: [text](url)
```

### Citations

Use footnotes or inline links:

```markdown
According to Smith, "quote here."[^1]

[^1]: Smith, Jane. *Book Title*. Publisher, 2020.
```

Or:

```markdown
According to [Smith](link), "quote here."
```

For academic work, include bibliography at end.

## Licensing and Attribution

### Original Work

By contributing, you agree to license your work under:
- **CC-BY-SA 4.0** (Creative Commons Attribution-ShareAlike) for essays, guides, and text
- **CC0** (Public Domain) for manifestos, exercises, and community resources
- **Specify license** for artwork (we recommend CC-BY or CC-BY-SA)

You retain copyright. We ask only for permission to publish and share.

### Attribution

**If you want credit**: Include your name, handle, or website at end of contribution

**If you want anonymity**: Indicate this clearly, and we'll publish without attribution

**Pseudonyms welcome**: Use any name you choose

### Derivative Work

When building on others' work:
- Clearly indicate what's original vs. adapted
- Credit sources properly
- Respect original licenses
- Link to source material

## Submission Process

### Via GitHub Pull Request (Preferred)

1. **Fork the repository** to your account
2. **Create a new branch** for your contribution: `git checkout -b add-essay-on-topic`
3. **Add your file(s)** in appropriate directory
4. **Commit with clear message**: `git commit -m "Add essay on terminal aesthetics"`
5. **Push to your fork**: `git push origin add-essay-on-topic`
6. **Open pull request** with description of contribution
7. **Respond to feedback** if requested
8. **Celebrate** when merged!

### Via Email

If you're not comfortable with GitHub:

1. **Email** your contribution to: [create appropriate email]
2. **Include**:
   - Your work (attach or paste)
   - Preferred attribution (name, pseudonym, or anonymous)
   - Which section it belongs in
   - Any context or notes
3. **Wait for response** (typically within 1 week)

### Via Issue

For smaller contributions:

1. **Open an issue** on GitHub
2. **Paste content** or describe contribution
3. **Tag appropriately**: `contribution`, `essay`, `resource`, etc.

## Review Process

### Timeline

- **Initial response**: Within 1 week
- **Substantive review**: Within 2-3 weeks for longer works
- **Minor edits**: Typically quick turnaround

### Criteria

We evaluate contributions on:

**Relevance**: Does it fit the project's themes and goals?

**Quality**: Is it well-written, researched, and thoughtful?

**Originality**: Does it add something new or valuable?

**Accessibility**: Can non-experts understand it?

**Accuracy**: Are facts correct and sources reliable?

**Respect**: Does it engage others' ideas fairly?

### Possible Outcomes

- **Accept**: Published as submitted (or with minor copyedits)
- **Revise and resubmit**: Accepted pending specific changes
- **Decline**: Doesn't fit project scope or meet quality standards

We provide feedback for all outcomes.

---

## Editorial Standards

### Writing Quality

- Clear, engaging prose
- Defined terms and concepts
- Logical organization
- Proofread for typos and grammar
- Appropriate tone (formal academic to personal essay)

### Factual Accuracy

- Verify facts before submission
- Cite sources for claims
- Acknowledge uncertainty where appropriate
- Update if errors are discovered

### Inclusivity

- Avoid assumptions about readers' backgrounds
- Use inclusive language
- Consider global/non-Western perspectives
- Make technical content accessible

### Respect

- Engage ideas, not people
- Critique systems, not individuals (except public figures in public roles)
- Avoid personal attacks
- Acknowledge legitimate disagreements

## Community Guidelines

### Code of Conduct

We're committed to providing a welcoming environment for contributors of all backgrounds. We will not tolerate:

- Harassment or intimidation
- Hate speech or discriminatory language
- Personal attacks or ad hominem arguments
- Trolling or bad-faith engagement
- Spam or promotional content unrelated to project
- Violations of others' privacy

**Violations** will result in removal of contributions and potential ban from project.

### Constructive Engagement

We encourage:

- Thoughtful critique and disagreement
- Questions and clarifications
- Suggestions for improvement
- Sharing diverse perspectives
- Building on others' ideas
- Giving credit generously

## Specific Contribution Types

### Adding to Galleries

1. Place work in appropriate gallery folder
2. Update COLLECTION.md with entry
3. Include:
   - Title
   - Artist name (or anonymous)
   - Year created
   - Dimensions/specifications
   - Brief description
   - Technical details

### Adding Case Studies

1. Research your subject thoroughly
2. Interview participants if possible
3. Locate primary sources
4. Structure: background, development, analysis, significance
5. Include images/examples with attribution
6. List sources at end

### Adding Toolkits

1. Test all instructions yourself
2. Include prerequisites clearly
3. Provide examples and variations
4. Link to tools and resources
5. Consider different skill levels
6. Invite feedback and improvements

### Adding Resources

1. Verify all links work
2. Write brief, accurate annotations
3. Place in appropriate category
4. Alphabetize within category (for books, authors)
5. Note if resource requires payment or registration

## Rights and Permissions

### Using Others' Work

**Public domain**: Use freely with attribution

**Creative Commons**: Follow license terms (usually attribution required)

**Copyrighted**: Obtain permission or use fair use (brief quotes with citation)

**Interviews**: Get written consent to publish

**Images**: Use only if you have rights or permission

**Code**: Respect software licenses

When in doubt, ask for permission or don't use it.

### Your Rights

You retain full rights to your contributions. We ask only for:
- Non-exclusive right to publish
- Right to make minor edits (formatting, typos)
- Right to translate to other languages

You may publish your work elsewhere simultaneously or subsequently.

## Recognition

### How We Credit Contributors

- **Essays**: Byline at top or bottom of piece
- **Artwork**: Artist credit in caption and COLLECTION.md
- **Manifestos**: Signatories listed at end
- **Interviews**: Both interviewer and subject credited
- **Toolkits**: Author credited at end
- **Translations**: Translator credited prominently
- **Minor contributions**: Listed in commit history

### Contributors List

Significant contributors will be added to a CONTRIBUTORS.md file (coming soon) acknowledging their work.

### Anonymous Contributions

Completely respected. No pressure to reveal identity.
