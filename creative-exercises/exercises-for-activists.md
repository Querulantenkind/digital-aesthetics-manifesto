# Creative Exercises for Activists

## Introduction

These exercises explore technology, creativity, and activism—using digital tools for resistance, organizing, consciousness-raising, and building alternative futures.

Each exercise combines practical action with reflective practice, treating activism as creative work that requires imagination, strategy, and solidarity.

---

## Exercise 1: The Zine as Organizing Tool

### Concept
Create a one-page zine addressing a local issue, distribute physically and digitally.

### Materials
- Single sheet of paper (folds into 8-page booklet)
- Photocopier or printer
- Markdown for digital version
- Distribution plan

### Instructions

1. **Identify issue**: Local, specific, actionable
2. **Research**: Facts, personal stories, demands
3. **Design**: Text + visuals, clear hierarchy
4. **Production**: Print 50-100 copies
5. **Distribute**: Leave in public spaces, hand to people, post online

### Template Structure

```
Page 1 (cover): Bold title + image
Page 2-3: Problem explained
Page 4-5: Personal impact / stories
Page 6-7: Demands / solutions / actions
Page 8 (back): Resources / contact info
```

### Variations

- **Series**: Issue one zine per month for a year
- **Collaborative**: Different people write different pages
- **Multilingual**: Same zine in multiple languages
- **Response zine**: Reply to someone else's zine

### Reflection Prompts

- Who did you reach that other media wouldn't?
- What's the power of print in digital age?
- How does DIY aesthetic signal authenticity?
- What feedback did you receive?

## Exercise 2: The Anonymous Drop

### Concept
Create and distribute information anonymously—leaving traces that raise questions.

### Materials
- Printer
- Public spaces
- Anonymous online accounts (optional)
- Safety awareness

### Instructions

1. **Create artifact**: Poster, sticker, pamphlet, QR code
2. **Message**: Clear, provocative, actionable
3. **Anonymize**: No identifying information
4. **Place strategically**: High-visibility, relevant locations
5. **Document**: Photograph placements (safely)
6. **Observe**: Watch for reactions

### Example Artifacts

**Stickers**:
```
┌─────────────────────────┐
│  YOUR RENT IS PROFIT    │
│     FOR SOMEONE ELSE     │
│                          │
│    ORGANIZE. RESIST.     │
└─────────────────────────┘
```

**QR codes** linking to:
- Know Your Rights information
- Mutual aid networks
- Organizing resources
- Alternative media

**Posters**:
ASCII art + message, photocopied, wheat-pasted

### Variations

- **Projection mapping**: Project messages on buildings
- **Chalk**: Temporary street art
- **Digital drops**: Anonymous info in forums/comments
- **Dead drops**: USB sticks with information left in public

### Safety Considerations

- **Don't**: Leave anything dangerous or illegal
- **Do**: Avoid cameras, wear gloves, plan exit routes
- **Know**: Local laws about flyers/graffiti/trespassing
- **Consider**: Who might be harmed by this action?

### Reflection Prompts

- What's the power of anonymity in activism?
- How does mystery amplify message?
- What are the ethics of anonymous action?
- Did your message spread beyond your placements?

## Exercise 3: The Teach-In Materials

### Concept
Create educational materials that can be printed, copied, and shared for a teach-in or workshop.

### Materials
- Research on topic
- Design tools
- Markdown/LaTeX for clean formatting
- Print-friendly design

### Instructions

1. **Choose topic**: Something you know well or will research
2. **Create handout**: 1-2 pages, information-dense
3. **Design for copying**: Black and white, clear hierarchy
4. **Include actions**: What can people do with this info?
5. **Test**: Ask friend if it's clear and actionable

### Template Structure

```markdown
# [TOPIC]: A Quick Guide

## What's Happening?
[2-3 paragraphs explaining issue]

## Why It Matters
- Bullet points of impacts
- Personal stories
- Data/statistics

## What You Can Do
1. Immediate actions (call rep, sign petition)
2. Ongoing engagement (join org, donate)
3. Long-term work (organize, educate others)

## Resources
- Organization contacts
- Websites
- Further reading
- Legal support

## About This Handout
Created by [you/org]. Copy freely. Updated [date].
```

### Topics to Address

- Digital privacy and surveillance
- Labor rights in tech industry
- Climate and computing
- Algorithmic bias
- Accessibility as justice
- Internet censorship
- Open source as resistance

### Variations

- **Zine**: Longer, more narrative
- **Infographic**: Visual data presentation
- **FAQ**: Question and answer format
- **Timeline**: Historical context

### Reflection Prompts

- Is your handout accessible to non-experts?
- What actions are realistic for most people?
- How can materials be continuously updated?
- Who benefits from this knowledge?

## Exercise 4: The Data Visualization for Justice

### Concept
Create an ASCII/text-based data visualization that reveals injustice or inequality.

### Materials
- Data (public datasets, FOIA requests, research)
- Programming language or spreadsheet
- ASCII art skills
- Clear message

### Instructions

1. **Find data**: Inequality in income, arrests, pollution, etc.
2. **Analyze**: What story does the data tell?
3. **Visualize**: Create chart/graph in ASCII
4. **Annotate**: Add context, sources, interpretation
5. **Share**: Social media, zines, posters

### Example (Income Inequality)

```
INCOME DISTRIBUTION IN CITY NAME (2023)

Top 10%    ████████████████████████████████ $250k avg
           ↑ (320% increase since 1980)

Middle 50% ████████ $58k avg
           ↑ (12% increase since 1980)

Bottom 40% ██ $22k avg
           ↓ (-5% decrease since 1980)

Each █ = $10k average annual income
Source: Census Bureau, analyzed by [org]
```

### Variations

- **Time series**: Show change over time
- **Comparison**: Two groups side by side
- **Geographic**: ASCII map with data overlaid
- **Network**: Connections between entities (funding, ownership)

### Reflection Prompts

- Does visualization make injustice clearer?
- How does ASCII/text make data more accessible?
- Can simple graphics be more powerful than complex ones?
- How to avoid misrepresenting data?

## Exercise 5: The Email Campaign Template

### Concept
Create a template that makes it easy for others to take action via email.

### Materials
- Text editor
- Research on targets (elected officials, companies, etc.)
- Clear ask

### Instructions

1. **Identify target**: Who has power to create change?
2. **Research**: Find email addresses, staff names
3. **Write template**: Formal, clear, personalized
4. **Include fields**: [YOUR NAME], [YOUR STORY], etc.
5. **Distribute**: Post publicly, share in organizing spaces

### Example Template

```
Subject: Constituent Request: Support Bill [NUMBER]

Dear [REPRESENTATIVE NAME],

My name is [YOUR NAME] and I'm a constituent in [DISTRICT]. 
I'm writing to urge you to support Bill [NUMBER], which would 
[BRIEFLY DESCRIBE BILL].

[PERSONAL STORY: 2-3 sentences about how this affects you]

I ask that you:
1. Vote YES on Bill [NUMBER]
2. Co-sponsor the legislation
3. Speak publicly in support

This issue affects [NUMBER] people in our district. I will be 
watching your vote on this matter.

Thank you for your service.

Sincerely,
[YOUR NAME]
[YOUR ADDRESS - shows you're a constituent]
[YOUR PHONE NUMBER - optional]
```

### Variations

- **Phone call script**: Same content, conversational tone
- **Social media post**: Public pressure template
- **Letter campaign**: Physical mail version
- **Petitions**: Longer statement with signatures

### Reflection Prompts

- How do templates balance efficiency and personalization?
- What makes an effective ask?
- How to measure impact?
- When is collective voice more powerful than individual?

## Exercise 6: The Security Culture Zine

### Concept
Create educational materials about digital security for activists.

### Materials
- Research on security practices
- Threat modeling knowledge
- Clear, non-condescending writing
- Distribution plan

### Instructions

1. **Assess audience**: What threats do they face?
2. **Prioritize advice**: Most important practices first
3. **Write clearly**: No jargon, assume no prior knowledge
4. **Test usability**: Can someone follow your instructions?
5. **Keep updated**: Security advice changes

### Topics to Cover

**Digital Security**:
- Encrypted messaging (Signal, etc.)
- Password management
- Two-factor authentication
- VPN use
- Secure email
- Anonymous browsing (Tor)

**Physical Security**:
- Protest safety
- Legal observer training
- Know your rights
- Dealing with police/security

**Cultural Security**:
- Operational security (OPSEC)
- Trust and vetting
- Compartmentalization
- Secure communication norms

### Template Section

```markdown
## Encrypted Messaging

### What:
Apps that scramble messages so only sender and receiver can read them.

### Why:
Protects private conversations from surveillance, hacking, and data breaches.

### How:
1. Download Signal (iOS/Android)
2. Verify safety numbers with contacts
3. Enable disappearing messages
4. Turn off message previews on lock screen

### When:
Use for ANY organizing communications, even if "nothing to hide."

### Warning:
- Still vulnerable if device is compromised
- Metadata (who talks to whom) may be logged
- Screenshots can be taken and shared
```

### Variations

- **Digital security workshop**: Multi-session curriculum
- **Security audit**: Checklist for organizations
- **Incident response**: What to do if compromised
- **Platform-specific**: Security for Twitter, Facebook, etc.

### Reflection Prompts

- How to communicate urgency without fear-mongering?
- What's the balance between security and usability?
- How to keep guides current?
- Who's most vulnerable and what do they most need?

## Exercise 7: The Speculative Zine

### Concept
Create a zine from an imagined future where your movement succeeded.

### Materials
- Imagination
- Design tools
- Hope

### Instructions

1. **Imagine victory**: What does it look like?
2. **Write from future**: News articles, personal stories, statistics from that world
3. **Be specific**: Details make it believable
4. **Design**: Make it look like a real artifact from that future
5. **Share**: Inspiration for movement

### Example Content

```markdown
# THE COOPERATIVE CHRONICLE
## May 15, 2030 - Issue #127

### Community Land Trust Celebrates 5th Anniversary
"We never thought we'd see the day where housing 
was truly affordable..."

### Tech Worker Union Reaches 80% Membership
After three years of organizing...

### City Council Votes to Decommodify Internet Access
Free municipal broadband for all residents...

### In Memoriam: Remembering the 2025 Rent Strike
The largest coordinated action in city history...
```

### Variations

- **News from the revolution**: Ongoing struggle
- **History textbook**: Looking back 50 years
- **Personal diary**: Individual experiencing better world
- **Graphic novel**: Visual storytelling

### Reflection Prompts

- How does envisioning victory shape strategy?
- What details made the future feel real?
- How to balance utopia and realism?
- Did this exercise generate new ideas?

## Exercise 8: The Protest Aesthetics

### Concept
Design a cohesive visual identity for a protest or movement.

### Materials
- Design skills
- Understanding of movement values
- Reproducibility in mind

### Instructions

1. **Research movement**: Values, history, symbols
2. **Design system**: Colors, fonts, imagery, slogans
3. **Create templates**: Signs, banners, social graphics
4. **Document**: Style guide for others to use
5. **Share freely**: Open-source the design

### Design Elements

**Visual Identity**:
```
Colors: [Primary], [Secondary], [Accent]
Fonts: [Bold sans-serif for impact]
Symbols: [Specific imagery, icons]
Layout: [Grid system, alignment rules]
```

**Templates**:
- Protest signs (8.5"×11", 11"×17")
- Banners (6'×3')
- Social media graphics (1080×1080, 1920×1080)
- Stickers (3"×3")
- T-shirt designs

**Slogans**:
- Short, chantable
- Rhythmic
- Specific demands
- Inclusive language

### Example (Climate Strike)

```
Colors:
- Earth Blue #1B4D77
- Forest Green #2D5016
- Warning Orange #FF6B35

Fonts:
- Headings: Impact (bold, all caps)
- Body: Arial (clear, legible)

Key Visual:
- Earth silhouette
- Clock showing "11:59"
- Rising temperature graph

Slogans:
- "System Change, Not Climate Change"
- "There Is No Planet B"
- "Listen to the Science"
```

### Variations

- **Historical revival**: Adapt designs from past movements
- **Counter-branding**: Subvert corporate aesthetics
- **Accessible**: Design for low vision, colorblind
- **Multilingual**: Same design, multiple languages

### Reflection Prompts

- How does visual unity strengthen movement?
- What makes a symbol recognizable?
- How to balance aesthetics and accessibility?
- What's the relationship between style and message?

## Exercise 9: The Mutual Aid Network Map

### Concept
Create a resource map of local mutual aid—services, organizations, contacts.

### Materials
- Research (interviews, web search, personal knowledge)
- Simple mapping tool or hand-drawn map
- Distribution plan

### Instructions

1. **Inventory resources**: What exists in your community?
2. **Map it**: Geographic or categorical organization
3. **Include info**: Contact, hours, services, access notes
4. **Design for sharing**: Print-friendly, updatable
5. **Distribute**: Community centers, libraries, online

### Categories

- Food (pantries, fridges, co-ops)
- Housing (shelters, housing support, tenant unions)
- Healthcare (free clinics, harm reduction)
- Legal (know your rights, free legal aid)
- Technology (free wifi, computer access, repair cafes)
- Community spaces (libraries, makerspaces, gardens)
- Organizing groups (unions, tenant orgs, activist groups)

## Exercise 10: The Solidarity Statement Generator

### Concept
Create a template system for quickly drafting solidarity statements.

### Materials
- Text editor
- Knowledge of effective statements
- Distribution list

### Instructions

1. **Analyze good statements**: What makes them effective?
2. **Create template**: Fill-in-the-blank structure
3. **Write instructions**: How to use template
4. **Test**: Draft statement for recent event
5. **Share**: With organizations, coalitions

### Template Structure

```markdown
# Solidarity Statement Template

## Header
[YOUR ORGANIZATION] stands in solidarity with [GROUP/INDIVIDUAL]

## Context
On [DATE], [WHAT HAPPENED]. This action is part of a broader 
pattern of [SYSTEMIC ISSUE].

## Condemnation
We condemn [SPECIFIC ACTION/POLICY] and [WHO IS RESPONSIBLE].

## Solidarity
We stand with [AFFECTED COMMUNITY] in their struggle for 
[SPECIFIC DEMANDS].

## Our Commitment
[YOUR ORGANIZATION] commits to:
1. [SPECIFIC ACTION 1]
2. [SPECIFIC ACTION 2]
3. [SPECIFIC ACTION 3]

## Call to Action
We call on [TARGET AUDIENCE] to:
- [ACTION 1]
- [ACTION 2]
- [ACTION 3]

## Closing
In solidarity,
[YOUR ORGANIZATION]
[CONTACT INFO]
[DATE]

## Signatories (if coalition)
- [ORG 1]
- [ORG 2]
- [ORG 3]
```

### Variations

- **Press release**: For media
- **Internal statement**: For members
- **Social media**: Shortened version
- **Letter to officials**: Formal tone

### Reflection Prompts

- What makes solidarity meaningful vs. performative?
- How to center affected communities?
- When to speak and when to listen?
- How does public solidarity build power?

## Collaborative Exercises

### Group Exercise 1: The Skillshare

**Participants**: 5-20 people

**Process**:
1. Everyone teaches one skill (30 minutes each)
2. Skills can be: technical, political, practical, creative
3. Document workshops
4. Share materials publicly

**Outcome**: Distributed knowledge, deskilled hierarchies

---

### Group Exercise 2: The Solidarity Campaign

**Participants**: 2-3 organizations

**Process**:
1. Identify shared issue
2. Co-design campaign
3. Divide responsibilities
4. Execute together
5. Debrief and document

**Outcome**: Coalition building, shared power

---

### Group Exercise 3: The Creative Action

**Participants**: Affinity group (5-15 people)

**Process**:
1. Brainstorm creative tactics (art, performance, theater)
2. Choose action
3. Plan logistics, safety, message
4. Execute
5. Document and share

**Examples**: Flash mobs, die-ins, banner drops, guerrilla projections

## Safety and Ethics

**Before Any Action**:
- Threat assessment: What are the risks?
- Consent: Does everyone agree?
- Backup plans: What if things go wrong?
- Debrief: Learn from every action

**Ethical Considerations**:
- Center those most affected
- Punching up, not down
- Long-term vs short-term wins
- Building power, not just awareness
